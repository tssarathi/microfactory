# The Good Trades Co. — Plumbing Troubleshooting: Drainage and Grease Traps

## Safety Precautions

- **PPE required:** Wear chemical-resistant gloves, safety glasses, boot covers, and a face shield when working on drainage systems. Wastewater contains biological hazards including bacteria, viruses, and parasites.
- **Biological hazard awareness:** Treat all wastewater as infectious. Wash hands thoroughly after any contact. If wastewater contacts eyes or open wounds, flush with clean water immediately and seek medical advice.
- **Ventilation:** Ensure adequate ventilation when working on drainage systems, especially in enclosed or below-ground spaces. Sewer gases (hydrogen sulphide, methane) can accumulate in confined areas. If confined space entry is required, follow The Good Trades Co. confined space entry procedure and use a 4-gas detector.
- **No chemical drain cleaners in grease trap systems.** Chemical cleaners (caustic soda, sulphuric acid) damage grease trap components, kill the biological breakdown process, and may violate local trade waste agreements. Use mechanical clearing methods only.
- **Electrical safety:** When using an electric eel or high-pressure jetter, ensure the equipment is tested and tagged, and the power supply is protected by an RCD.

## Blockage Diagnosis

### Step 1 — Initial Assessment

1. Identify the affected fixtures — is it a single fixture (localised blockage) or multiple fixtures (main drain blockage)?
2. Check the nearest inspection opening (IO) or overflow relief gully (ORG). If water is overflowing at the ORG, the blockage is downstream.
3. Ask the customer when the problem started, whether it is intermittent or constant, and whether there have been recent plumbing works.

### Step 2 — Camera Inspection

1. Insert a drain camera at the nearest access point to identify the blockage location and cause.
2. Record the distance from the entry point to the blockage. WO-9 (commercial kitchen drain) found a partial restriction at **3.2 m** from the entry point.
3. Identify the blockage type:
   - **Fat/grease/food buildup** — most common in commercial kitchens.
   - **Tree root intrusion** — common in older outdoor drainage, visible as fibrous mass in the pipe.
   - **Debris/foreign objects** — wet wipes, sanitary products, food scraps.
   - **Pipe damage** — cracked, displaced, or collapsed pipe sections require repair, not just clearing.
4. Check the pipe condition during the camera run — note any cracks, joint displacement, or pipe material deterioration for the customer report.

### Step 3 — Clear the Blockage

Select the clearing method based on the blockage type and pipe size:

**Electric Eel:**
- Use a **32 mm** cutter head for standard branch drains (basin, shower, floor waste). WO-9 used a 32 mm electric eel to clear the blockage.
- Use a **50 mm** cutter head for main drains and larger diameter pipes.
- Feed the cable steadily — do not force. If resistance is met, pulse the motor and work through the blockage gradually.
- Retrieve all debris cleared from the pipe.

**High-Pressure Jetter:**
- Use for compacted grease blockages that the electric eel cannot fully clear.
- Select the appropriate nozzle for the blockage type (forward-firing for solid blockages, rear-firing for grease).
- Start with lower pressure and increase as needed. Do not exceed the pipe's pressure rating.

### Step 4 — Flush and Verify

1. After clearing, flush the line with **hot water (80C)** for a minimum of 15 minutes to melt and flush remaining grease from pipe walls. WO-9 performed a 15-minute hot water flush at 80C.
2. Run a post-clearing camera inspection to verify the blockage is fully cleared and the pipe is undamaged.
3. Flow test all affected fixtures — water should drain freely without backing up.

## Grease Trap Maintenance

### Inspection and Cleaning Schedule

- **Monthly** for commercial kitchens — this is a minimum. High-volume kitchens may require fortnightly service.
- WO-9 found the grease trap at **90% capacity**, only 6 weeks after the last clean — it should have been cleaned monthly. When the trap reaches 75% capacity or higher, schedule immediate cleaning.

### Cleaning Procedure

1. Remove the grease trap lid carefully. Allow gases to dissipate before working over the trap.
2. Pump out the grease trap contents using a vacuum truck or portable pump. WO-9 removed **45 L of waste** from the trap.
3. Scrape the walls, baffles, and base of the trap to remove adhered grease.
4. Flush the inlet and outlet pipes to clear any grease buildup in the connected pipework.
5. Inspect the trap body for cracks, baffle damage, and seal condition.
6. Replace the lid and verify it seals correctly.

### Waste Disposal

- All grease trap waste must be disposed of by a **licensed waste carrier**. The Good Trades Co. uses **GreenWaste QLD** for all grease trap waste disposal.
- Obtain a waste disposal receipt from the carrier for every pump-out. Retain receipts for the customer's records — local council may audit trade waste compliance.
- Never dispose of grease trap waste into stormwater, general waste bins, or onto ground.

### Capacity Thresholds

| Level | Action |
|---|---|
| < 50% | Normal — no action required |
| 50-75% | Monitor — schedule cleaning within 2 weeks |
| 75-100% | Clean immediately — risk of discharge into drainage system |
| Overflowing | Emergency — clean immediately, check downstream drains |

## Prevention

### Drain Strainer Baskets

- Install strainer baskets in all prep area sinks to catch food waste before it enters the drain. WO-9 installed a drain strainer basket in the prep sink as a preventive measure.
- Strainer baskets should be emptied into general waste bins at the end of each shift — never into the sink.

### Staff Training

- Advise the kitchen manager on proper oil/fat disposal practices:
  - Pour used cooking oil into designated waste drums, not into sinks.
  - Scrape plates and pots into bins before washing.
  - Do not pour hot grease down any drain.
- WO-9 specifically advised the kitchen manager on these points, including: (1) grease trap needs monthly cleaning, (2) staff training on proper oil/fat disposal into waste drums not sinks.

### Quarterly Professional Maintenance

- The Good Trades Co. commercial kitchen drainage maintenance contracts include quarterly service visits.
- Quarterly service covers: grease trap pump-out and clean, drain line camera inspection, preventive hot water flush of all kitchen drains, strainer basket condition check, and customer report with recommendations.
- WO-9 booked the next quarterly maintenance service for the customer with a due date of 2026-06-01.

### Hot Water Flushing

- Regular hot water flushing (weekly by kitchen staff, 80C for 5 minutes through each drain) helps prevent grease accumulation between professional services.
- This is a supplementary measure — it does not replace professional grease trap cleaning.

## Common Blockage Patterns by Location

| Location | Common Cause | Typical Clearing Method |
|---|---|---|
| Prep sink drain | Food waste, grease | 32 mm electric eel |
| Floor waste (kitchen) | Grease, soap, food | 32 mm electric eel + hot flush |
| Main kitchen drain | Compacted fat/food/soap | 32 mm or 50 mm eel + jetter |
| Grease trap outlet | Grease overflow from full trap | Pump out trap first, then eel |
| External drain | Root intrusion, ground movement | 50 mm eel or jetter, camera |

## Documentation

For every drainage and grease trap job, record:

1. Blockage location (distance from entry point in metres).
2. Blockage cause (grease, roots, debris, pipe damage).
3. Clearing method used (eel size, jetter, hand clearing).
4. Volume of waste removed from grease trap (litres).
5. Pre and post camera inspection findings.
6. Preventive measures installed or recommended.
7. Waste disposal receipt reference number (from GreenWaste QLD).
8. Next service due date.

## When to Escalate

- **Pipe damage** (cracked, displaced, or collapsed pipes) — requires excavation and pipe replacement by a licensed plumber with council notification.
- **Root intrusion from council infrastructure** — contact the local council for tree root management on council property.
- **Trade waste compliance issues** — if a customer's grease trap is undersized for their operation or consistently overflowing despite monthly cleaning, they may need a larger trap. This requires engineering assessment and council trade waste approval.
- **Hazardous materials in drainage** — if chemical contamination is suspected (unusual colours, smells, or reactions), stop work and contact The Good Trades Co. supervisor.
